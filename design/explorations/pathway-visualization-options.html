A · Horizontal stepped ladder A literal staircase. Five labeled rungs from "First sail" to "Olympic podium," each annotated with age range or competitive level. The peak rung uses the rust accent. Static, diagrammatic, scannable in a glance.
Why thisMost communicative in the least space. Reads as a credential — "this academy has thought about every rung." Pairs naturally with section 2's pillar grid (each pillar maps to rungs).
RiskDiagrams can read as corporate / brochure-y. Won't carry emotional weight on its own — needs photography or a voiceover to make it feel earned.

B · Vertical scroll journey Five numbered moments stacked vertically, each with its own photograph and short description. Reads top-to-bottom as the visitor scrolls. Most editorial, most cinematic.
Why thisEach rung gets enough room to feel real — photography does most of the work, copy fills in. Builds emotional momentum down the page; the final rung lands as a payoff.
RiskTakes vertical real estate. Ladder is harder to grasp at a glance — visitors who skim will see "five photos," not "the through-line."

D · Named alumni at each rung The ladder is a real person. Same sailor's name walked up four rungs over four years, with quotes at each stage. Closes with their current achievement. Most credibility-driven and most emotional.
Why thisThe pathway becomes evidence, not aspiration. "We've done this for them — we can do it for your kid." Strongest possible answer to "is this academy real?" Pairs perfectly with the heritage section.
RiskRequires a real alumnus willing to be the named example. If you can name 3-4 actual sailors, this is the strongest option in the deck. If not, it's a writing exercise.

Pick a treatment. D is the strongest if real alumni are sourceable — turns the academy claim into receipts. B is the safest cinematic option. A is a good "explanatory" base that could be paired with B (diagram up top, journey below).
