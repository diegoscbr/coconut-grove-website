Hero subhead · what goes under the headline

The headline is locked (option C below). Now we need the supporting line — the sentence that answers "what do they actually do?" Mouratoglou's equivalent: "More than 100 nations. From beginners to top professionals." Short, declarative, sets up the rest of the page.

THE PREMIER
SAILING ACADEMY
IN BISCAYNE BAY
Since 1946 · Accessible · World-Class
From a child's first tack to the Olympic podium — the full pathway, on the same water, with the same standards.
A · Pathway / progression Leads with the through-line: one continuous ladder from learn-to-sail through elite racing. "Same water, same standards" is the promise — accessibility doesn't mean a watered-down version of the program. Strong if the homepage's main story is the pipeline.
Year-round programs for juniors, adults, and racing teams — from learn-to-sail to international competition.
B · Breadth / audiences Most explicit. Names every audience and bookends the level range. Closest to how a club's "Programs" page would describe itself. Reads as a clear billboard but loses some poetry. Best if the goal is "no one wonders if this place is for them."
World-class coaching on the warmest training waters in North America — for sailors from age six to the Olympic podium.
C · Coaching + territory Foregrounds the two non-negotiable assets: the people (Maru-tier coaching) and the place (Biscayne Bay's year-round sailable conditions). "Age six to Olympic podium" carries the breadth without listing every audience. Closest to Mouratoglou's voice.
For 80 years, the Bay's training ground for sailors. Today, the academy where the next generation learns to win.
D · Heritage → future Two-beat structure: heritage anchor, then the new ambition. Honors the 1946 fact already in the headline modifier and signals the Maru-era shift without naming her. Risk: "learns to win" leans hard on competition; might feel narrow next to the "accessible" claim above.
