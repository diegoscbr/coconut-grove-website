A · Uniform 3+2 grid Five same-size cards. Photo on top, name + 1-liner + arrow CTA. Standard practice — Mouratoglou's exact treatment, every modern academy site, every product grid you've ever seen.
Why thisMost predictable. Lowest cognitive load. Every pillar reads as equally important — accessibility-first signal.
RiskReads as a menu, not a brand statement. The new HPC arm gets visually equal billing with summer camps.

B · Featured HPC + 4-grid High Performance Center as a wide hero card on top — image, eyebrow ("The new chapter"), longer description, Maru's name. Other four pillars in a uniform row below.
Why thisVisually announces the new arm without burying it in a uniform grid. Section 2 doubles as both program menu and "what's new here" statement.

C · Image-as-background, name-only Five vertical tiles, all same size. Each is a full-bleed photograph with a numbered counter (01–05) and the pillar name overlaid bottom-left in display weight. No description on the card — hover or tap reveals more.
Why thisMost cinematic and most distinctive. Lets the photography carry the meaning — which works because you're already cutting a custom video for the hero. Mouratoglou's most modern variant.
RiskDemands five strong photographs (one per pillar). Until those exist, this approach is fragile. Also less explanatory — first-time visitors might not know what "Camps & Coaching" actually offers.

D · Editorial split Section title and intro paragraph live in a left column; programs are a vertical stack of horizontal rows on the right (small image + title + 1-liner). Reads like a magazine spread.
Why thisMost editorial / magazine-feeling. Lets you actually tell visitors what's here in the section intro. More room for meaningful descriptions per program.
RiskLess visual energy — the programs themselves get smaller real estate. Mobile collapses the split, which loses the editorial effect.
