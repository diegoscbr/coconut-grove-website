Sample CTA"Apply" · "Sign up" · "Show up" · "Sail with us"
Voice rules
  • Short sentences. 6-12 words average.
  • Imperative. Tell, don't suggest.
  • Active verbs. Sail, race, train, win.
  • Specific. Name the regatta, the boat, the date.
  • Light contractions. "We'll" not "we will."
Don't
  • Marketing fluff: "world-class," "passion-driven."
  • Hedging: "might consider," "potentially."
  • Long subordinate clauses.
A · Coach voice The way Maru actually talks on the water. Closest to how the academy operates day-to-day.
Why thisMost credible — voice matches the work. Distinctive against tourism-brochure sailing-club copy. Easy to maintain across writers (the rules are simple).
RiskCan feel curt for the heritage section ("80 years. Twelve Olympians.") — heritage benefits from more breath. Too much imperative everywhere reads like commands.

Sample CTA"Read the brief" · "Find your program" · "Walk through the pathway"
Voice rules
  • Variable length. Mix 8-word sentences with 30-word ones.
  • Subordinate clauses for nuance.
  • Em dashes for emphasis — like this.
  • Specific imagery. "The dock" not "our facility."
  • Confident, not boastful. State, don't sell.
Don't
  • Adverbs that prop up adjectives ("incredibly elite").
  • Passive voice for accountability ("mistakes were made").
  • Generic sailing-romance ("the call of the sea").
B · Editorial voice The tone of a strong sailing magazine — Outside, The Atlantic's adventure cover stories, Patagonia's catalog.
Why thisMost brand-distinctive. Reads as crafted, not templated. Matches the heritage + ambition arc — both deserve real writing. Pairs well with the Inter type system.
RiskHardest to maintain across writers. One unedited paragraph can drag the whole site. Requires an editor on every piece.

Sample CTACoach voice always. "Apply." · "Sign up." · "Visit." · "Watch."
Voice rules
  • Headers + CTAs: coach voice (A's rules).
  • Body paragraphs: editorial voice (B's rules).
  • UI labels: coach. ("Calendar," "Programs," "Visit.")
  • Stories & profiles: editorial.
  • Calendar entries: coach. (Date, name, age range.)
Don't
  • Mix the two within a single paragraph.
  • Use editorial voice on a button.
  • Use coach voice in a heritage essay.
C · Hybrid — coach + editorial Surface reads punchy and scannable; deep reading rewards the visitor with crafted prose. Matches how the page is actually consumed (skim → dive).
Why thisBest of both — fast surfaces for buyers, depth for readers. Lets calendar/CTAs stay terse while heritage/stories breathe. Most flexible across page types.
RiskTwo voices to maintain. Writers need to know which mode applies where. Needs a clear style guide, not just principles.
D · Receipts voice
Every claim backed by a number, a name, or a date
No adjective without evidence.

Sample CTAAlways specific: "Apply by July 15" not "Apply now."
Voice rules
  • Number, name, or date in every claim.
  • No "world-class" — name the credentials.
  • No "many" — give the count.
  • No "soon" — give the date.
  • Receipts in CTAs. "Apply by July 15," not "Apply now."
Don't
  • Vague intensifiers: "highly," "extremely."
  • Empty superlatives: "the best," "premier" (in body — headline is OK).
  • Adjectives without proof.
D · Receipts voice Every body sentence earns its place with a fact. Like reading a Patagonia field report or a Boeing spec sheet.
Why thisMost disciplined and most distinctive. Protects against marketing-speak drift over time. Builds credibility section-over-section as facts accumulate. Pairs naturally with Coaching D's stat board.
RiskReads cold if not warmed by photography and design. Heritage and personal stories struggle in this voice — needs editorial relief in profile pieces.

Pick a voice. C is the most flexible across page types and matches how visitors actually consume the page (skim then dive). D is the most disciplined and self-protecting against drift. A and B are pure-form bets.
