Mobile treatment

Mobile is the majority case for this audience — parents researching on the go, sailors checking the calendar, kids browsing programs. The decision: how much to redesign for mobile vs. let the desktop simply reflow. Four strategies.

Mobile context Majority traffic. Parent doing research on phone, sailor checking calendar between classes, kid showing parent. Inquiry form (section 8) is the conversion goal — everything else routes there.

A · Standard reflow Hamburger menu top-right. Sections stack 1-up. Same content as desktop, narrower. Inquiry form lives only at the bottom.
Why thisCheapest to build. Familiar pattern. No new mobile-specific decisions to debate.
RiskLong scroll to reach inquiry form. Visitors who land mobile may bounce before they ever see the conversion moment.

B · Hamburger + sticky bottom CTA A + a persistent rust bar at the bottom: "Visit / Inquire →" Always visible, always tappable. Anchors to section 8.
Why thisConversion path is one tap from anywhere on the page. Easiest mobile add to ship. Doesn't require new IA — just a global sticky element.
RiskSticky CTA covers ~40px of every screen — always visible can become always invisible. Some visitors will tune it out.

C · Bottom tab navigation No hamburger. Bottom tab bar with 4 sections (Programs / Calendar / Stories / Visit). Search icon replaces hamburger top-right. App-like.
Why thisMost thumb-friendly. Reduces taps for the conversion path. Reads as a real product, not a brochure. Native-app energy.
RiskLess brand-traditional — websites with tab nav read as "app" which can feel off-brand for a 1946 institution. Pillars don't all fit (5 pillars compressed into 4 tabs requires "Programs" to be the umbrella).

D · Accordion-heavy Sections render as collapsed accordions on mobile. Hero stays visible; everything below is tap-to-expand. Sticky CTA at bottom.
Why thisFastest scan — visitor sees the entire page at-a-glance, expands what they care about. Saves vertical space dramatically. Very different from desktop, which reads as intentional mobile design.
RiskHides content by default — bad for SEO if rendered client-side, fine if SSR'd with collapse via JS. Some visitors hate accordions; bounce rate could rise if executed poorly.

Pick a strategy. B is the conservative-but-conversion-aware choice. C is the most thumb-friendly. A is the cheapest. D is the most distinctive but most opinionated.

Hybrid likely: most academy sites end up with A's reflow + B's sticky CTA — that's a defensible default if you want me to lock it.
